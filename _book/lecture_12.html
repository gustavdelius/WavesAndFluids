12  Bernoulli’s principle and vorticity

You find content related to this lecture in the textbooks:

  • Batchelor (2000) sections 1.3, 1.4, and 1.5

Solving Euler’s equations is hard. It is therefore worthwhile to first try to derive consequences of the equations without actually solving them. So in this lecture we will use our vector calculus skills to manipulate Euler’s equations a bit until they reveal to us Bernoulli’s principle and the vorticity equation.

12.1 Bernoulli’s principle

We will make two observations that allow us to rewrite the equations in new ways.

  1. Because gravity is a conservative force, it can be written as minus the gradient of a potential: \(\underline{g}= -\nabla\chi\). In the simple case where gravitational force is constant and in the negative \(z\) direction, \(\chi = g\,z\) so that \(\underline{g} = -g\,\underline{e}_z\). Hence, Eq. 11.11 can also be written as \[ \frac{D\underline{u}}{Dt} = - \nabla \left(\frac{p}{\rho} + \chi\right). \tag{12.1}\]

  2. We will use the vector calculus identity \[ (\underline{u}\cdot\nabla)\underline{u} = (\nabla\times\underline{u})\times\underline{u}+\nabla\left(\frac{u^2}{2}\right), \tag{12.2}\] which holds for any differentiable vector field \(\underline{u}\) and thus in particular for the velocity field. Using this, together with the definition of the material derivative, in the left-hand side of Eq. 12.1 gives \[\begin{split} \frac{D\underline{u}}{Dt} &=\partial_t\underline{u}+(\underline{u}\cdot\nabla)\underline{u}\\ &=\partial_t\underline{u}+(\nabla\times\underline{u})\times\underline{u}+\nabla\left(\frac{u^2}{2}\right)\\ &=- \nabla \left(\frac{p}{\rho} + \chi\right). \end{split} \tag{12.3}\] This we can rewrite as \[ \partial_t\underline{u}+(\nabla\times\underline{u})\times\underline{u} = - \nabla \left(\frac{p}{\rho} + \frac{u^2}{2} + \chi\right). \tag{12.4}\] In this form the Euler equation involves the curl of \(\underline{u}\) which is known as the vorticity \[ \underline{\omega}=\operatorname{curl} \underline{u} = \nabla\times\underline{u}. \tag{12.5}\] If we also introduce the function \[ H=\frac{p}{\rho} + \frac{u^2}{2} + \chi, \tag{12.6}\] sometimes known as Bernoulli’s integral, we can write the equation simply as \[ \partial_t\underline{u}+\underline{\omega}\times\underline{u} = - \nabla H. \tag{12.7}\]

This simplifies even further if we consider a steady steady flow, so that \(\partial_t\underline{u}=0\). Then Eq. 12.7 simplifies to \[ \underline{\omega}\times\underline{u}=-\nabla H. \tag{12.8}\] To get rid of the vector product in this equation, we take the dot product with \(\underline{u}\) on both sides: \[ \underline{u}\cdot(\underline{\omega}\times\underline{u})=-\underline{u}\cdot\nabla H. \] On the left hand side the cross product \(\underline{\omega}\times\underline{u}\) is orthogonal to \(\underline{u}\) so that the dot product with \(\underline{u}\) is zero. So the right-hand side is zero: \(\underline{u}\cdot\nabla H=0\). Together with the fact that for a steady flow \(\partial_t H\) is zero, this shows that the material derivative of \(H\) is zero: \[ \frac{DH}{Dt}=\partial_tH+\underline{u}\cdot\nabla H=0. \] So \(H\) does not change along pathlines or, which for steady flows is the same thing, along streamlines. This gives us

To keep \(H\) constant, higher velocity must correspond to lower pressure, and vice versa.

Next we restrict ourselves to flows where there is no vorticity, \(\underline{\omega} = 0\). Such flows are called irrotational flows. In this case Eq. 12.8 implies that \(\nabla H=0\), which together with \(\partial_t H=0\) gives us

This theorem is used to explain the lift created by the airflow around an aerofoil: because the air flows faster along the top of the aerofoil than along the bottom, there is a lower pressure above the aerofoil than below. 12.2 Vorticity

The vorticity \(\underline{\omega}\) of a flow with the velocity field \(\underline{u}\) is defined as the of \(\underline{u}\) (see Vector Calculus notes, p. 26): \[ \underline{\omega}=\operatorname{curl} \underline{u} = \nabla\times\underline{u}. \tag{12.9}\] An example will make it easier to understand how to best think of vorticity

Example 12.1 We consider the very simple flow \(\underline{u}=(\alpha y, 0, 0)\) for some constant \(\alpha>0\). This flow is parallel to the \(x\) axis, with the magnitude of the velocity increasing with increasing \(y\). The streamlines are simply straight horizontal lines parallel to the \(x\) axis. These are also the pathlines because this is a steady flow. So nothing flows in circles, so one might be tempted to guess that this is an irrotational flow. However the calculation shows that \[\begin{split} \underline{\omega}&= \nabla\times\underline{u}=(\partial_y\underline{u}_z-\partial_z\underline{u}_y, \partial_z\underline{u}_x-\partial_x\underline{u}_z, \partial_x\underline{u}_y-\partial_y\underline{u}_x)\\ &=(0,0,-\partial_y\underline{u}_x)=(0,0,-\alpha)\neq \underline{z}. \end{split}\] So there is a non-zero vorticity – the flow is not irrotational. To see this intuitively, imagine placing an extended object into the flow. If you want to do this in your bathtub, I recommend gluing two matches together to form a cross and place this on the water surface. If you now create a flow, this object may start to rotate. It will do so in the flow in this example because the upper end of the object finds itself in an area of faster flow while the lower end is in an area of slower flow, so the object will rotate clockwise.

12.2.1 The evolution of vorticity

To derive an equation which governs the evolution of vorticity, we take the \(curl\) of Eq. 12.7 . \[ \nabla\times\partial_t\underline{u}+\nabla\times(\underline{\omega}\times\underline{u})=-\nabla\times\nabla H. \] Using the fact that \(curl\) of \(grad\) is zero, we obtain \[ \partial_t\underline{\omega} + \nabla\times(\underline{\omega}\times \underline{u}) = \underline{z}. \tag{12.10}\] We will now use the general vector calculus identity \[ \nabla\times(\underline{a}\times\underline{b})=(\nabla\cdot\underline{b})\underline{a}+(\underline{b}\cdot\nabla)\underline{a}-(\nabla\cdot\underline{a})\underline{b}-(\underline{a}\cdot\nabla)\underline{b} \] which holds for any two differentiable vector fields \(\underline{a}\) and \(\underline{b}\). Applying this with \(\underline{a}=\underline{\omega}\) and \(\underline{b}=\underline{u}\) gives \[ \partial_t\underline{\omega} + (\nabla\cdot\underline{u})\underline{\omega}+(\underline{u}\cdot\nabla)\underline{\omega}-(\nabla\cdot\underline{\omega})\underline{u}-(\underline{\omega}\cdot\nabla)\underline{u}= \underline{z}. \] Using the incompressibility condition \(\nabla\cdot\underline{u}=0\) and the fact that the \(div\) of a \(curl\) is zero and thus \(\nabla\cdot\underline{\omega}=0\), the above simplifies to \[ \partial_t\underline{\omega} + (\underline{u}\cdot\nabla)\underline{\omega} = (\underline{\omega}\cdot\nabla)\underline{u}. \tag{12.11}\] Using the definition of the material derivative we obtain the

Vorticity equation

\[ \frac{D\underline{\omega}}{Dt} = (\underline{\omega}\cdot\nabla)\underline{u}. \tag{12.12}\]

Note that the pressure \(p\) does not appear in the vorticity equation.

This simplifies for a 2D flow \(\underline{u}=(u_x(x,y,t), u_y(x,y,t),0)\). In the calculation of the vorticity most terms vanish leaving only \[ \underline{\omega}=\nabla\times\underline{u}=(0,0,\omega)~~~\text{ with } \omega = \partial_x u_y-\partial_y u_x. \] Then \[ (\underline{\omega}\cdot\nabla)\underline{u} = \omega \, \partial_z \underline{u}=\underline{z} \] because there is no \(z\) dependence in the 2D flow. As a result, the vorticity equation Eq. 12.12 ) reduces to \[ \frac{D\omega}{Dt}=0. \tag{12.13}\] Thus:

If we assume that far away in front of the airfoil the air is still and thus described by a flow with no vorticity, then the vorticity stays zero along the paths of all the air molecules also as the aerofoil passes. Thus the entire flow is irrotational and we can apply Bernoulli’s theorem for irrotational flows to argue that the airfoil experiences a lift force. All we still need to establish is that the velocity is higher above the airfoil than below. That is going to occupy us for the next three lectures.
